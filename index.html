This casebook is a comprehensive annual report documenting a total of 62 incidents, compiled through an exhaustive survey of personal data breaches discovered between January and December 2025 and resolutions passed at Personal Information Protection Commission (PIPC) plenary sessions from 2025 to early 2026.

Key Findings

#Management failures caused more damage than technical attacks
1Failure to revoke former employees' access caused more than half of all damage. Coupang (33.7M) + Netmarble (6.11M) — just two incidents affected approximately 39.8 million people, resulting not from sophisticated hacking but from the absence of a basic procedure: revoking credentials at offboarding.
2SQL injection was the most frequent cause of PIPC sanctions (8+ cases). This most basic web attack, known since the 1990s, continued to recur in 2025, with most affected companies lacking WAF deployment and input validation.
3Article 29 (security measures obligation) served as the legal basis in 22 out of 23 confirmed fine cases (96%), making it effectively the sole legal ground for administrative fines. This demonstrates that the failure to maintain "basic" technical safeguards is the structural root cause of personal data breaches in South Korea.
4Dwell times were extreme. SK Telecom: 4 years; GPKI: 3 years; National Research Foundation of Korea: 12 years — many incidents were not "caused" in 2025 but merely "discovered" in 2025.
5All three major telecom carriers (SKT, KT, LGU+) were hacked, and all exhibited transparency issues (concealment, suspicion of evidence destruction, delayed reporting). This suggests a structural vulnerability across the entire industry, not isolated corporate failures.
6SaaS supply chains emerged as a new attack vector. Three LVMH subsidiaries were breached sequentially via the same SaaS pathway within three months (combined fines: KRW 36 billion; 5.5 million victims), demonstrating that security perimeters must extend beyond internal infrastructure to encompass the entire supply chain.

Research Background and Legal Framework

The 2023 Comprehensive Amendment to PIPA and the Significance of 2025

The Personal Information Protection Act (Act No. 19234), amended in March 2023 and effective March 15, 2024, represents a turning point in South Korea's personal data protection framework. The key changes are as follows.

Raised fine ceiling3% of violating entity's revenue (substantially strengthened from the former regime of tens to hundreds of millions of KRW per violation type). This made the record-setting KRW 134.8 billion fine against SK Telecom possible.
Shortened breach notification deadlineChanged from "without delay" → within 72 hours for both data subject notification and PIPC reporting. Mandatory 72-hour reporting applies to breaches involving 1,000+ persons, sensitive/unique identifier data, or external unauthorized access. Among 2025 incidents, Dior, Tiffany (13 days), Coupang (28 days), and Modetour (2 months) violated this deadline and received combined Article 34 sanctions.
New cross-border transfer regulationsNew Article 28-8 — restricts cross-border transfers without data subject consent or adequate safeguards. PIPC first applied this provision to AliExpress (KRW 1.978 billion, July 2024), followed by the KRW 1.37 billion fine against Temu in 2025.
Strengthened PIPC independenceThe PIPC, established in 2020, transitioned to its second term (August 2023–) with enhanced investigation and sanctioning capabilities. In 2025, it recorded an unprecedented enforcement volume of 27 resolutions across 26 plenary sessions.

In short, 2025 was the inaugural year in which the full enforcement results of the amended Act materialized. The total fines of KRW 202.3 billion represent a leap of several dozen times compared to pre-2024 annual fine totals (in the low billions of KRW), a direct product of the institutional transformation driven by the legislative amendment.

Effective September 11, 2026: PIPA 4th Amendment (Punitive Fines)

In direct response to the cascade of major breaches in 2025, the 4th amendment to the Personal Information Protection Act, which passed the National Assembly plenary on February 12, 2026 and was promulgated on March 10, 2026, will take effect on September 11, 2026. This amendment — for which the incidents documented in this casebook served as direct legislative motivation — represents the most powerful strengthening of sanctions in the history of South Korean personal data protection.

Punitive finesIntroduction of a special provision raising the fine ceiling from 3% to 10% of total revenue for repeated or serious violations. Applicability criteria: (1) repeated willful or grossly negligent violations within the past 3 years, (2) large-scale damage affecting 10 million or more persons, (3) incidents occurring after failure to comply with corrective orders.
* Had the SK Telecom incident occurred after this law took effect, fines could have reached approximately KRW 400 billion.
Enhanced CEO/CPO accountabilityDesignates the business owner or representative as the ultimate person responsible for the safe processing of personal information and the protection of data subject rights. CPOs are required to manage professional staff, secure budgets, and report to the representative and board of directors. CPO qualification: at least 4 years of combined experience in personal information protection, information security, and information technology (minimum 2 years in personal information protection required).
Changed notification triggerThe trigger is moved forward from "upon confirmation of breach" to "upon becoming aware of the possibility of breach." Notification scope expanded to include forgery, alteration, and destruction. Expected to prevent recurrence of cases like Coupang (28-day delay) and KT (1.5-year concealment).
Mandatory ISMS-P certificationMandates ISMS-P (Information Security and Personal Information Protection Management System) certification for companies exceeding certain thresholds of revenue and personal data processing volume. Effective from July 1, 2027.

2026 Amendment to the Information and Communications Network Act (Concurrent Implementation)

Alongside the Personal Information Protection Act, the Information and Communications Network Act was also amended to strengthen the cyber incident response framework.

Fines for repeat breachesFines of up to 3% of revenue may be imposed when two or more cyber incidents occur within 5 years due to willful or gross negligence. Directly applicable to repeat violators such as GS Retail (3 incidents using the same method within 4 years) and LG Uplus (incidents in 3 consecutive years).
Compulsory compliance penalties for obstructing investigationsCompulsory compliance penalties of 0.03% of daily average revenue for failure to comply with corrective orders, refusal to submit materials, or obstruction of investigations during cyber incident inquiries. Provides a direct sanctioning basis for evidence destruction such as LG Uplus's server decommissioning and KT's server disposal.

Methodology

1. Scope Definition

This casebook covers a total of 62 incidents, comprising personal data breaches in South Korea that were discovered or disclosed between January 1 and December 31, 2025 (Section I, 49 cases) and sanctions for legacy incidents that occurred between 2020 and 2024 but were resolved at PIPC plenary sessions between 2025 and early 2026 (March) (Section II, 13 cases). Supplementary cases (#37–#46) are separately noted as an extension of Section I.

2. Data Collection

Primary sources (official records): An exhaustive survey of all 26 plenary sessions held by the Personal Information Protection Commission (PIPC) between January 2025 and March 2026 was conducted to collect resolutions, press releases, and corrective orders. PIPC press release URLs are directly linked in pipc.go.kr nttId= format, with policy briefing originals (korea.kr) also cited.

Secondary sources (specialized media): Articles from six specialized outlets — Boannews, DailySECU, Edaily, Digital Times, ET News, and ZDNet Korea — were collected for cross-verification. The principle was to confirm at least two independent sources per incident; where only a single source existed, the source rating was downgraded accordingly.

3. Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria

Included: (a) Incidents where the leakage of data qualifying as "personal information" under PIPA was confirmed or reasonably presumed, (b) sanction cases confirmed by PIPC plenary session resolutions or official press releases, (c) pre-resolution incidents reported by multiple media outlets with significant scale or social impact.
Excluded: (a) Simple system failures where no personal data breach occurred, (b) incidents occurring overseas with no confirmed impact on Korean users, (c) cases under litigation where factual circumstances are unresolved and no media coverage exists.

4. Classification Framework

Each incident was classified along three axes.

Attack type (10 categories): SQL injection, credential stuffing, ransomware, web shell/web vulnerability, insider breach, APT/state-sponsored, supply chain attack, SaaS/cloud compromise, misconfiguration/illegal collection, other.

Industry (7 categories): IT/platform, finance/insurance, retail/e-commerce, manufacturing/energy, public sector/education, healthcare/welfare, foreign companies.

Threat actor (4 categories): External attacker (unspecified), state-sponsored attacker (North Korea, etc.), insider (employee/outsourced personnel), third-party/supply chain intermediary.

5. Source Reliability Ratings

A three-tier reliability rating was assigned to each of the 62 incidents.

★★★ Primary source — 36 cases: Cases confirmed by official records including PIPC resolutions, government press releases, and joint investigation team announcements. Highest reliability for figures and factual claims.

★★☆ Reliable reporting — 19 cases: Cases based on cross-verified reports from two or more major media outlets. Pre-official resolution, but factual circumstances substantially confirmed.

★☆☆ Single source / Unconfirmed — 7 cases: Cases based on a single media report or under ongoing investigation. Figures and details are subject to change, as noted in the relevant entries.

6. Three-Stage Analysis Framework

Each incident was analyzed using the following three-stage framework.

(1) What Happened: Objectively describes the chronological progression of the incident, attack vector, types and scale of leaked data, and PIPC sanctions. Specifies the violated PIPA provisions and verifies compliance with breach notification requirements.

(2) Engineering Vulnerability Analysis (How It Happened): Technically reconstructs the attack kill chain to analyze the initial intrusion vector, privilege escalation path, lateral movement, and data exfiltration mechanism. Presents relevant CVEs, MITRE ATT&CK techniques, and defensive architecture alternatives.

(3) Structural Causes and Institutional Implications (Why It Happened): Analyzes structural causes beyond technical vulnerabilities, including economic incentive structures, regulatory gaps, organizational culture, and personnel shortages. Derives recurring patterns through cross-referencing among similar incidents and proposes institutional improvements.

7. Limitations

(a) No access to private forensics: This casebook was prepared solely based on publicly available sources (PIPC resolutions, media reports). As internal forensic reports, incident response records, and non-public investigation materials from each company were not accessible, the engineering analysis includes reasonable inferences based on available information.

(b) Inferences included: The attack kill chain reconstructions in the "Engineering Vulnerability Analysis" sections construct plausible scenarios based on fragmentary public information (types of leaked data, attack vector names, vulnerability types). Actual attack paths may differ.

(c) Pending cases subject to change: The 7 cases rated ★☆☆ and some cases rated ★★☆ are under ongoing PIPC investigation; the scale of damage, violated provisions, and fines may change upon final resolution. Figures in this casebook are current as of March 25, 2026.

Note: The monthly timeline is based on incident "discovery/disclosure" dates, which may differ from the actual attack initiation dates (see dwell time analysis).

Background
Summary: From 2024.12.27 to 2025.1.4, attackers used ID/password combinations leaked from other sites to conduct automated credential stuffing attacks against the GS25 "My Refrigerator" app, gaining unauthorized access to approximately 90,000 accounts. GS Retail detected the anomalous logins internally and voluntarily reported to PIPC on January 6. The compromised data included 7 categories — name, gender, date of birth, phone number, address, user ID, and email — but did not include payment information or passwords.

Response: GS Retail blocked the offending IP addresses, reset passwords, and individually notified affected customers. However, it was subsequently discovered that the same attack had been ongoing for 8 months on GS Shop (#02), another GS Retail service, during the same period.

Cross-reference: Same attack method — #02 GS Shop, #08 T-money, P.01 Secta Nine. All 5 credential stuffing cases in 2025 share the common root cause of absent anomaly detection systems.
Technical Analysis
Technical Context: Credential stuffing exploits users' password reuse habits, with bots automatically testing millions of ID/password combinations. GS25 lacked basic bot defense mechanisms including login attempt rate limiting, CAPTCHA, and suspicious IP range blocking. An identical credential stuffing incident had already occurred at GS25 in 2021, drawing criticism that recurrence prevention measures had been inadequate for 4 years.

Engineering Vulnerability Analysis — Credential Stuffing Attack Vector: Credential stuffing involves mass-injecting ID/password combinations leaked from other sites using automation tools (Sentry MBA, OpenBullet, STORM, etc.). The GS25 mobile app's login API was exploited through three key vulnerabilities. First, no rate limiting was applied to the API endpoint, allowing dozens to hundreds of login attempts per second from a single IP to pass through unblocked. Second, the absence of bot detection mechanisms such as CAPTCHA or device fingerprinting meant the system could not distinguish automated tool requests from legitimate users. Third, the lack of an account lockout policy based on failed login thresholds allowed unlimited attempts. These three defense layers are specified as minimum requirements in the OWASP Credential Stuffing Prevention Cheat Sheet. Had rate limiting been applied per IP and per session at the API Gateway level, along with real-time monitoring for anomalous login patterns (multiple account attempts from the same IP, geographically impossible logins, etc.), the large-scale breach could have been prevented.
Structural Implications
Structural Causes and Institutional Implications: The recurrence of credential stuffing at GS25 (see Summary) is not mere negligence by an individual company but an inevitable outcome produced by the security investment structure of Korea's retail industry. In the revenue model of convenience store apps (point accumulation, mobile coupons, pickup services), security is a cost item that does not directly generate revenue, and app performance KPIs (login response under 2 seconds, conversion rate optimization) directly conflict with friction elements like rate limiting and CAPTCHA. In an organizational structure where the mobile app team is evaluated on UX metrics and the security team has no visible achievements when no incidents occur, proposals to add bot detection to the login API are rejected as "conversion rate decline" — an immediate cost — versus "preventing incidents that may not occur" — an uncertain benefit. The fact that GS Retail faced minimal actual sanctions after the 2021 incident reinforced this economic calculus. If the case was concluded with corrective recommendations without fines, from a rational manager's perspective, the implementation cost of rate limiting (tens of millions to hundreds of millions of won including infrastructure changes, QA, and app update cycles) becomes "an insurance premium that need not be paid." This calculus applies equally to all 6 credential stuffing cases in 2025 (GS Shop #02, T-money #08, Secta Nine, KT Alpha, Korea Student Aid Foundation). KISA's ISMS certification criteria require user password complexity under the "password management" category but do not explicitly include API-level bot detection, rate limiting, or behavior-based authentication as mandatory certification items for service providers. This contrasts with EU PSD2's SCA (Strong Customer Authentication) requirements, which mandate real-time transaction monitoring and dynamic linking for service providers. To break the structural recurrence of credential stuffing in Korea, it is necessary to add API endpoint-specific bot detection and anomalous authentication pattern monitoring as mandatory items in ISMS certification criteria, and to require actual detection logs as evidence during certification renewal. Voluntary security investment has been proven to fail through 6 identical incidents over 5 years.

Background
Summary: During the investigation of the GS25 incident (#01), it was additionally discovered that GS Shop (home shopping), another service under the same GS Retail group, had also been subject to credential stuffing ongoing for approximately 8 months (approximately 240 days) since 2024.6.21. GS Retail filed an additional report with PIPC on 2025.2.13. The compromised data was similar to GS25, including name, gender, date of birth, phone number, address, email, and purchase history.

Impact Scale Comparison: GS25 (8 days, 90,000 persons) vs GS Shop (240 days, 1.58 million records) — time to detection is the critical variable that determines the scale of damage. Credential stuffing damage is nearly proportional to the duration of the attack.

Outlook: Under PIPC investigation. GS Retail has accumulated a total of 3 incidents using the same attack method — 2021 (GS25) + 2024–2025 (GS25 · GS Shop) — making it potentially subject to aggravated fines (up to 3% of revenue ceiling) for repeat violations within 3 years.
Technical Analysis
Core Issue — 8 Months Undetected: Despite the attack persisting for approximately 240 days, GS Shop's security systems completely failed to detect it. This indicates (1) absence of automated alerts for daily login failure/success rates, (2) absence of logic to block mass login attempts from anomalous IP ranges, and (3) non-operation of log analysis/SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) systems. The fact that two services under the same company suffered from the same attack method simultaneously constitutes a group-level security governance failure.
Structural Implications
Engineering Vulnerability Analysis — Structural Causes of 8-Month Non-Detection: The fact that credential stuffing went undetected at GS Shop for 8 months suggests structural defects in the log architecture beyond simple monitoring absence. In a normal security operations environment, a SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) would generate alerts within hours through correlation analysis of login failure rate spikes, multi-account access from the same IP, and anomalous User-Agent patterns. Eight months of non-detection means (1) login event logs were not integrated into the SIEM, or (2) no SIEM existed at all, or (3) detection rules did not include credential stuffing patterns. Furthermore, the fact that GS25 (#01) and GS Shop (#02) operate separate authentication systems despite being under the same group (GS Retail) while security policies were not centrally managed demonstrates the problem of security governance fragmentation across subsidiaries and services of large corporate groups. Had a unified authentication (SSO) architecture been adopted, attack patterns detected on one side would have been immediately propagated as blocking rules to other services.

Structural Implications

Structural Causes and Institutional Implications: The SK Telecom incident carries the deepest structural implications of all 2025 incidents. Analysis: Structural Causes Behind South Korea's Dwell Time Exceeding the Global Average by 10x

The phenomenon of Korean organizations' dwell times far exceeding the global average stems not from a single cause but from a multi-layered structure.

First, a structural absence of detection capabilities. The primary factor behind the reduction in global dwell times in Mandiant's statistics is the proliferation of active threat detection solutions such as EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response), XDR (Extended Detection and Response), and MDR (Managed Detection and Response). However, EDR adoption among Korean SMEs and mid-sized companies is significantly lower compared to large enterprises (approx. 60–70%), and in particular, Black Yak, Incruit, National Research Foundation of Korea, and Busan International Finance Center—all featured in this casebook—either did not operate basic IDS/IPS or did not perform log monitoring. Without detection tools, attackers can dwell indefinitely.

Second, an absolute shortage of security personnel. According to KISA's 2024 Information Security Survey, fewer than 20% of companies with under 250 employees have dedicated security staff. Even when security monitoring (SOC) is outsourced, the majority operate business-hours-only monitoring (8x5) rather than 24x7 to reduce costs. In SK Telecom's case, despite being a large enterprise, anomalous indicators on the HSS (Home Subscriber Server)—critical infrastructure—went undetected for 4 years, presumably because the USIM authentication server was excluded from the security monitoring scope.

Third, lack of incentives for breach reporting. Another factor contributing to the global decline in dwell times is GDPR's 72-hour notification requirement and the heavy fines for non-compliance. South Korea also introduced a 72-hour notification requirement through its 2023 amendment, but as demonstrated by KT (1 year 6 months of concealment), Coupang (28-day delay), and Mode Tour (2-month delay) in the 2025 cases, compliance rates remain low. The practice of delaying notification even after becoming aware of a breach not only distorts dwell time statistics but also deprives victims of the opportunity to prevent secondary harm.

Fourth, an immature threat intelligence sharing framework. In Mandiant's report, the shortest dwell times globally are cases where breaches were identified through external notification (law enforcement, security firms, peer organizations). Korea's C-TAS (Cyber Threat Analysis System) exists but lacks real-time IOC (Indicators of Compromise) sharing and automated response integration, meaning a threat detected at one organization does not translate into early detection at others. The possibility that BPFDoor malware used in the SK Telecom incident was detected at other Korean entities over the years—yet this intelligence was not relayed to SK Telecom—suggests a failure of the sharing framework.

In conclusion, South Korea's prolonged dwell times are the result of four overlapping structural deficiencies: 'absence of detection tools → shortage of security personnel → incentives to delay reporting → intelligence disconnection.' To approach global levels (10 days), MDR service deployment for SMEs, strengthened PIPC penalties for reporting delays, and establishment of a real-time automated IOC distribution system through C-TAS must proceed in parallel.

INSIGHT
Pattern Analysis & Insights
Insight 1
The Cost Asymmetry Paradox — A Structure Where Companies Don't Invest Even When Defense Costs Less Than Fines

The most paradoxical pattern repeatedly confirmed across 62 incidents is that companies do not invest in defense despite pre-emptive defense costs being overwhelmingly cheaper than post-incident sanctions. The case of Busan International Finance Center (P.13) illustrates this starkly. The annual operating cost of a firewall is approximately KRW 5 million. This institution went without a firewall for 4 years and was consequently fined KRW 95.4 million. The 4-year firewall operating cost of KRW 20 million versus the fine is a 4.8x ratio. In hindsight, this was a clearly irrational decision, but ex ante, it was rational. Why?

First, probability underestimation. The management of Busan International Finance Center likely judged that "the probability of a small institution like ours being hacked is extremely low." The expected cost of KRW 5 million for a firewall is a certain expenditure, but the expected cost of the KRW 95.4 million fine is "probability of occurrence x fine amount." If the hacking probability is assessed at less than 1%, the expected fine falls below KRW 950,000 — lower than the firewall cost. Second, absence of cyber insurance. Korea's cyber insurance market, at approximately KRW 20 billion in annual premiums as of 2024, is only 0.3% of the US market (~$7 billion). When insurance is active, insurers' underwriting enforces minimum security standards, but in Korea this market mechanism does not function. Third, insufficient deterrence from fine ceilings. Terastar's (P.08) KRW 5 million fine may be less than the cost of replacing a Windows Server. The 4th amendment (10% of revenue ceiling) taking effect in September 2026 may change this structure, but deterrence for micro-enterprises remains questionable.

Institutional reform direction: Breaking the cost asymmetry paradox requires three pillars. First, establishing a minimum fine floor to eliminate the perverse incentive that "even if hacked, the fine is cheaper than defense." Second, the Financial Services Commission and PIPC should jointly develop tax incentives for cyber insurance activation (premium tax credits) and insurer security underwriting guidelines. Third, PIPC should publicly disclose the "ratio of fines to prior security investment" when calculating administrative fines, providing companies with quantitative benchmarks for investment decision-making. The numbers — KRW 5 million for a firewall vs. KRW 95.4 million in fines — should not only be meaningful in hindsight but should become a tool that changes decisions in advance.

Insight 2
SI Vendor Monoculture — The Hidden Supply Chain SPOF Revealed by 8 SQL Injection Cases

When viewed individually, the 8 SQL injection-sanctioned cases (Black Yak, BusinessOn, NHN Witu, Korea Topic, NHN Commerce, EZEN, Douzone Housing, Leisure Plus) are different incidents at different companies. However, analyzing the technology stacks reveals a common pattern: the majority are web applications built by external SI vendors using similar technology stacks (PHP+MySQL or legacy Java/JSP). The simultaneous sanctioning of EZEN, Douzone Housing, and Leisure Plus in P.10 occurred because the same or similar SI vendors built multiple sites using identical code templates and architectures.

This is a different type of supply chain risk from SaaS supply chains (LVMH 3 companies). SaaS supply chain risk is the visible risk of "if one platform is compromised, all customers are affected," but SI monoculture is the invisible risk of "dozens of independent sites sharing the same code DNA share the same vulnerabilities." If SI Vendor A built 100 SME sites using PHP+MySQL, the moment SQL injection is discovered in one of them, the remaining 99 are extremely likely to have the same vulnerability. However, since these 100 sites are operated by different companies, there is no mechanism for one incident to trigger security reviews at other sites.

The structure of Korea's SI market exacerbates this monoculture. Small SI vendors compete on low-price contracts of tens of millions of KRW per project, reusing existing code templates to cut costs. Framework migration (PHP to Laravel, JSP to Spring Boot) increases development costs by 30–50%, resulting in elimination from price competition. Consequently, code DNA from the early 2010s continues to replicate and spread through 2025. This is identical to the risk of genetic monoculture in agriculture: a single pathogen (vulnerability) can wipe out the entire crop (site cluster).

Institutional reform direction: Resolving SI monoculture requires reform of the SI contracting structure itself. First, the Ministry of SMEs and Startups' standard SI contract should mandatorily include a "Secure Code Review" item, with automated verification (SAST/DAST) for at least the OWASP Top 10 vulnerabilities as a delivery condition. Second, KISA should introduce an SI vendor registration or security certification system, creating a structure where SI vendors failing to meet security standards are excluded from public and quasi-public sector contracts. Third, when SQL injection is discovered at one SI vendor, a "cascade audit" system should be established mandating batch inspection of all other sites built by that vendor.

Insight 3
Assume Breach = The Only Defense — KT Alpha (51 persons) vs ClassU (1.6M persons), the Single Variable Creating a 30,000x Difference

The most actionable finding derived from the analysis of 62 cases is this: Rather than preventing a breach, it is realistically more effective to reduce the value of data exposed during a breach to near zero. The most dramatic demonstration of this principle is the contrast between KT Alpha (P.04) and ClassU (P.04). Both companies were sanctioned in the same PIPC resolution. Both received the same credential stuffing attack. Yet KT Alpha's damage was 51 persons, while ClassU's was 1.6 million. An approximately 31,000x difference.

What made the difference was not a firewall, not EDR, not SIEM. It was pre-emptive masking. KT Alpha masked sensitive information such as resident registration numbers and card numbers in API responses, so even when attackers successfully logged in, the value of obtainable information was near zero. In contrast, ClassU stored resident registration numbers in plaintext and exposed them directly in API responses, allowing attackers to bulk-harvest 1.6 million resident registration numbers. Comparing technical implementation difficulty, pre-emptive masking requires adding a single regex-based substitution function at the API response layer. Development effort: 1–2 days. These 1–2 days of development could have reduced the damage from 1.6 million to 51 persons.

The same principle is confirmed in other incidents. iStro (P.08) was infected with ransomware but rapidly restored from offline backups and was exempted from fines. EZEN (P.10) stored 35,454 resident registration numbers unencrypted, all of which were exfiltrated — had AES-256 encryption been applied, the leaked data would have been meaningless byte strings. Lotte Card (#02) recorded CVCs (card verification codes) in plaintext in log files — had CVCs not been stored at all per PCI DSS standards (tokenization), the risk of card fraud would have been eliminated.

Institutional reform direction: To institutionally embed the Assume Breach principle, a shift in regulatory evaluation criteria is needed. First, the safety measures standards notification evaluation framework should shift from "existence of defensive measures" to "existence of measures to minimize damage upon breach." Breaches where encryption, masking, or tokenization was applied should not be treated the same as plaintext breaches, and quantified fine reduction criteria should be presented. Second, KT Alpha's pre-emptive masking case should be published as an official PIPC best practice, with industry-specific application guidelines distributed. Third, explicit criteria should be established making confirmed plaintext storage of high-risk data such as resident registration numbers and card numbers an automatic aggravating factor for fines. The clearest conclusion from 62 cases is that every system will eventually be breached, and what matters is reducing the value of data exposed during a breach to near zero.

Insight 4
Former Employee Keys = The Greatest Threat — 2 Cases Account for 60%+ of Damages, a Process Failure Not a Technology Failure

Coupang (#29) and Netmarble (#30), just 2 incidents, account for more than half of the total damage across all 62 cases. Coupang 33.7 million + Netmarble 6.11 million = approximately 39.8 million persons. Considering Korea's population of approximately 51 million, these 2 cases alone affected a scale equivalent to 78% of the entire population (including duplicates). The attack vector in these 2 cases was not SQL injection, ransomware, or APT. It was failure to revoke the access credentials of former developers.

In Coupang's case, a former developer retained code signing keys after departure. Using these keys, they accessed APIs for 5 months, collecting personal information of 33.7 million customers. In Netmarble's case, a former developer's system access privileges were not revoked, and data accumulated since 2003 on 6.11 million persons had not been destroyed for 20 years, amplifying the damage scale. Both incidents were caused not by sophisticated hacking techniques but by the absence of the most basic HR-IT integration processes.

Former employee key management is a technically solved problem. By integrating the HR system with the IAM (Identity and Access Management) system via API, the moment termination processing is completed in the HR system, all credentials (AD accounts, SSH keys, API keys, code signing keys, VPN certificates, cloud IAM roles) are automatically deactivated. All major IAM solutions — Okta, Azure AD, Google Workspace — provide this functionality by default. The problem is not the absence of technology but the absence of process linkage between HR and IT departments. In many Korean companies, termination processing is handled by the HR team while IT account management is handled separately by the IT team, with the two processes relying on manual requests without automation. The reality is that an employee who departed on Friday has their account deactivation request processed on Monday — during which time the former employee can exfiltrate data.

Institutional reform direction: To institutionally strengthen former employee key management, first, the PIPC safety measures standards notification should explicitly mandate "deactivation of all access privileges within 24 hours of resignation/termination." Second, for companies processing personal information of 10,000 or more individuals, HR-IAM automated integration should be mandated, with quarterly audits of inactive accounts (unused for 90+ days). Third, explicit criteria should be established making breach via former employee access an aggravating factor for fines, institutionally conveying the message that the most basic process failures cause the greatest damage. The fact that 2 cases account for 60%+ of total damage tells us that the priority for security investment is not cutting-edge solutions but the former employee checklist.

Insight 5
Mapping Regulatory Blind Spots — Companies Whose Names Alone Don't Reveal Their Regulatory Scope

When mapping the 62 incident companies to the regulatory framework, a surprisingly large number are found to sit in blind spots of existing regulation. Drawing this blind spot map reveals the structural gaps in Korea's personal data protection regulation.

Blind Spot 1: Non-financial institutions with "finance" in their name. Busan International Finance Center (P.13) has "finance" in its name but is not subject to regulation by the Financial Supervisory Service or the Financial Security Institute — it is a local public corporation. Neither the Financial Security Institute's Electronic Financial Supervisory Regulations nor the Financial Services Commission's information security standards apply. Result: 4 years without a firewall, 433 login attempts per minute unblocked. While the KRW 95.4 million fine is 19 times the firewall cost, due to the regulatory gap, no supervisory authority demanded this standard in advance.

Blind Spot 2: Mass personal data processors outside ISMS certification obligations. Only information and communications service providers above certain thresholds are obligated to obtain ISMS-P certification, while offline-centric businesses (golf courses, travel agencies, educational institutions) process personal information of hundreds of thousands while remaining outside ISMS-P certification scope. Hanyang CC and Seoul CC (P.09) are golf courses but held large volumes of members' resident registration numbers and credit card information. EZEN (P.10) is an educational institution that stored 35,454 resident registration numbers in plaintext but was not subject to ISMS-P certification. Modetour (P.03) is a travel agency that failed to destroy 3.16 million non-member data records for 11 years but was not classified as an information and communications service provider, so enhanced standards did not apply.

Blind Spot 3: Limits of extraterritorial application to foreign companies. PIPC's imposition of fines on Temu (#11, China), 2K Games (P.11, US), and Moncler (P.07, Italy) set precedents for extraterritorial application, but enforcement has limitations. In Moncler's case, it took 3 years and 9 months from the incident (Dec 2021) to the resolution (Sep 2025). Enforcement procedures for asset seizure within Korea upon non-payment are unclear, and enforcement is practically impossible when foreign companies have no Korean legal entity.

Blind Spot 4: Lending companies and small quasi-financial institutions. Corporate insurance agencies (#13) process customer data entrusted by insurance companies but are not subject to insurance company-level security standards. The scope of financial regulation is limited to "financial institutions," leaving non-financial institutions that process financial data in a blind spot.

Institutional reform direction: To eliminate regulatory blind spots, the basis of regulation must shift from "the nature of the institution" to "the nature and volume of data processed." First, ISMS-P certification obligations should be expanded from "information and communications service providers" to "all businesses processing personal information of 10,000 or more individuals." Second, technical inspections by the Financial Security Institute should be mandated for non-financial institutions processing financial data (local public corporations, insurance agencies, lending companies). Third, to ensure the effectiveness of extraterritorial application to foreign companies, mechanisms for restricting services within Korea upon fine non-payment (app store removal, payment blocking) should be codified. The existence of regulatory blind spots is not the absence of regulation but a design flaw in regulation, requiring redesign to match the reality of data processing.

Recommendations
Policy Recommendations — Structural Reform Proposals

Policy and practice recommendations based on 6 patterns derived from the analysis of 62 cases in this casebook. Focused on institutional and structural reforms beyond individual companies' technical responses.

Recommendation 1 — Mandatory Former Employee Access Management

Problem: 2 former employee cases caused half of all damages (~39.8M persons)

The Coupang and Netmarble incidents originated from simple management failures — not revoking signing keys and access privileges upon departure. Current law stipulates 'safety measures' broadly, but specific obligations for former employee management remain at the notification (Safety Measures Standards) level.

Recommendation: (1) Explicitly mandate deactivation of all access privileges within 24 hours of resignation/job change in the Safety Measures Standards notification, (2) Mandate annual dormant/former employee account cleanup audits, (3) Establish grounds for aggravated fines upon violation.

International precedent: NIST SP 800-53 PS-4 (Personnel Termination) requires revocation of all system access on the day of termination and is mandatory for US federal agencies. ISO 27001:2022 Annex A.6.5 (Responsibilities after termination) specifies the same requirement. Singapore's PDPA, amended in 2025, raised fines for access control violations to up to 5% of annual revenue.

Recommendation 2 — Industry-Specific Minimum Security Standards for Basic Vulnerabilities Such as SQL Injection

Problem: A 1990s attack is the most common cause of PIPC sanctions in 2025 (8+ cases)

Most SQL injection victims are small and medium-sized companies, where WAF costs and lack of security professionals are structural causes. Simply requiring 'safety measures' from individual companies cannot prevent recurrence.

Recommendation: (1) KISA and PIPC jointly establish industry/scale-specific 'minimum security checklists' (WAF, parameterized queries, MFA, etc.), (2) Expand WAF and security assessment vouchers for SMEs, (3) Designate checklist non-compliance as an aggravating factor under Article 34.

International precedent: The UK NCSC's Cyber Essentials defines 5 basic security controls (firewall, secure configuration, access control, malware protection, patch management) and mandates certification for government procurement participation. Australia's ASD Essential Eight categorizes 8 basic measures into Maturity Levels 1–3 to encourage phased implementation. Both frameworks include SQL injection defense (input validation, WAF) in their minimum standards.

Recommendation 3 — Establishing SaaS Supply Chain Security Accountability Framework

Problem: SaaS not directly managed by companies serves as a major infiltration path

The LVMH 3 companies and NHN Commerce incidents involved breaches via SaaS platforms, creating a structural problem where data processing responsibility becomes ambiguous among controllers, processors, and SaaS providers.

Recommendation: (1) Develop guidelines that clearly classify SaaS providers as personal data 'processors,' (2) Recommend mandatory security assessment (SOC 2, ISO 27001, etc.) when adopting SaaS, (3) Explicitly extend the scope of Article 26 (outsourcing management and supervision) to cloud and SaaS.

International precedent: GDPR Article 28 specifies contractual security obligations for processors, guaranteeing the controller's right to audit the processor's security measures before and after engagement. The 2024 EDPB Guidelines 07/2020 (interpreting Article 28) explicitly classifies cloud and SaaS as processors, clarifying the accountability framework. The US FTC expanded breach notification obligations to third-party apps (including SaaS) in its 2023 Health Breach Notification Rule amendment.

Recommendation 4 — Strengthening Sanctions for Breach Concealment and Evidence Destruction

Problem: All 3 major telecoms had transparency issues; reporting delays are routine

KT (1.5-year concealment), LG Uplus (server destruction), Coupang (28-day delay), Modetour (2 months), and others repeatedly violated breach notification obligations. Current penalties for Article 34 violations are in the range of millions to tens of millions of KRW, providing weak deterrence.

Recommendation: (1) Establish a 50% fine surcharge for concealment and evidence destruction (referencing GDPR Art. 83(2)(k)), (2) Introduce graduated sanctions proportional to reporting delay period (warning for exceeding 24 hours, administrative penalty for exceeding 7 days, administrative fine for exceeding 30 days), (3) Strengthen whistleblower (public interest reporter) protection.

International precedent: GDPR Article 83(2)(k) explicitly lists "degree of cooperation with the supervisory authority" as an aggravating/mitigating factor in fine calculation. The Irish DPC recognized Meta's violation of the 72-hour notification obligation in the Facebook breach (2021) and imposed an additional EUR 17 million (Mar 2022). The US SEC introduced cybersecurity disclosure rules (Reg S-K Item 106) in 2023, requiring listed companies to file 8-K disclosures of material cyber incidents within 4 business days, with securities law sanctions for violations.

Recommendation 5 — Strengthening Supervision of Legacy Data Destruction Compliance

Problem: Data from 10–20 years ago multiplied damage scales by tens of times

Netmarble (2003 data retained for 20 years), Modetour (3.16M non-member records from 2013 not destroyed), National Research Foundation of Korea (2013 vulnerability left unpatched for 12 years), NHN Commerce (10-year legacy). Failure to destroy data past retention periods was the direct cause of damage escalation.

Recommendation: (1) Explicitly designate 'additional damage from undestroyed data' as an aggravating factor when calculating fines for Article 21 violations, (2) Mandate automatic destruction of dormant accounts inactive for 5+ years (current 1-year separate storage → full destruction at 5 years), (3) Consider introducing personal data volume disclosure obligations.

International precedent: GDPR Article 5(1)(e) establishes "not kept longer than necessary for the purposes" (storage limitation) as one of its 6 core principles. The UK ICO fined TikTok GBP 12.7 million in April 2023 for processing children's (under 13) personal data without parental consent in violation of UK GDPR (inadequate age verification and data minimization principle violation). Japan's Act on the Protection of Personal Information Article 19 (formerly Article 27) stipulates that data should be "promptly endeavored to be deleted" after the retention period expires, but criticism exists that it remains a best-effort obligation — Korea can strengthen this to a mandatory obligation.

Recommendation 6 — Enhancing Effectiveness of SME Fines

Problem: Per-person fines differ by 2,800x depending on company size

Woori Card (75K persons, KRW 13.5B) vs Incruit (7.27M persons, KRW 460M) — the revenue-proportional fine system is powerful for large corporations, but for SMEs, fines in the tens of millions of KRW are effectively absorbed as a 'cost of doing business.'

Recommendation: (1) Consider introducing a minimum fine floor for SMEs (e.g., minimum 0.5% of revenue), (2) Strengthen corrective orders and publication orders instead of fine reductions (referencing the iStro case — fine exemption for rapid response, replaced with publication order), (3) Incentive system for fine reduction at next offense when security investment is confirmed.

International precedent: GDPR Article 83(2)(d) recognizes "measures taken to mitigate the severity of the violation's consequences" as a mitigating factor for fines. Under the GDPR framework, DPAs (supervisory authorities) can independently choose between corrective orders (Art. 58(2)(d)) and fines (Art. 83), enabling proportionality principle application where corrective orders and publication measures are imposed on SMEs instead of fines. The US FTC imposed on GoodRx (2023) a $1.5 million fine together with a consent order requiring 20 years of independent security audits, presenting a model that combines monetary sanctions with behavioral change.

Methodological Limitations

Coverage ScopeThis casebook covers only incidents resolved at PIPC plenary sessions and those reported in major media. The number of personal data breach reports in 2025 was 311 in January–September alone (PIPC announcement), with medical institutions alone reportedly exceeding 100 cases. Therefore, the 62 cases in this casebook represent an extremely small fraction of total breach incidents and are biased toward large-scale, high-profile cases (selection bias).
Confirmed vs UnconfirmedOf the 62 cases, 27 are confirmed by PIPC resolution (★★★), while the remaining 35 are based on media reports (★★☆/★☆☆). In particular, Coupang (33.7M vs 3,000 records) and YES24 (up to 20M) are pre-confirmation estimates.
Estimated FinesIndividual fines for the 3 LVMH companies (Dior, Tiffany, Louis Vuitton) are estimates reverse-calculated from the combined KRW 36B total ("~" notation). Exact individual allocations require verification from the original resolution text.
Time-LimitedAs of March 25, 2026. Once fines are confirmed for the 25 cases under investigation (Coupang, YES24, Netmarble, etc.), totals and statistics may change significantly.
Quantitative Analysis LimitationsIndustry and attack-type classifications are based on PIPC resolutions and media reports. Some incidents with compound causes (e.g., credential stuffing + internal vulnerability) were classified under a single type. Additionally, due to the large number of undisclosed incidents, the sample cannot be considered representative of the population, and statistical inference (correlation, regression analysis, etc.) was not performed due to sample size limitations.

Conclusion — The Lessons of 2025, The Choices of 2026

If one sentence runs through all 62 incidents, it is this: Korea's personal data breaches were not the result of sophisticated hacking, but of the absence of fundamentals.

Former employees' keys were not revoked. SQL injection, discovered in 1998, still could not be blocked in 2025. An intruder hid for 4 years without detection. Breaches were known but concealed. 9 out of 10 defensive measures cost KRW 0, yet they were not implemented.

The KRW 202.3 billion in administrative fines, 100+ million affected individuals, and 31 sanctioned companies recorded in this casebook are all events that have already occurred. These numbers become meaningful only when the same events are not repeated.

On September 11, 2026, punitive fines (10% of revenue) take effect. CEO and CPO personal liability is codified. ISMS-P certification becomes mandatory. The system is changing. But no matter how strong the system becomes, if former employee accounts are not deactivated on Monday morning, if Prepared Statements are not applied, if resident registration numbers are not masked in API responses — the same casebook will be produced in 2026.

Execute the fundamentals. That is the only lesson these 62 incidents leave behind.
